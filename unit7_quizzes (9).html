Unit 7 · Plants
7.1Plant Structure & Organization
Organs of Flowering Plants
  • Most flowering plants have a root system (made of roots) and a shoot system (made of stem and leaves)
  • Most typical plants feature 3 vegetative organs, concerned with growth and nutrition: roots, stems, leaves
    • Flowers, seeds, and fruits are involved with reproduction, not growth and nutrition
  • Roots: anchors plant in soil; absorbs water and minerals from the soil; produces hormones
    • Root hairs: projections from epidermal root-hair cells; greatly increase absorptive capacity of root
  • Stems: main axis of a plant that elongates and produces leaves
    • Nodes occur where leaves are attached to the stem
    • Internode is the region between nodes
    • Axillary buds can produce new branches of the stem (or flowers)
    • Stem also has vascular tissue that transports water and minerals
  • Leaves: major part of the plant that carries on photosynthesis; foliage leaves are usually broad and thin
    • Blade — wide portion of foliage leaf
    • Petiole — stalk attaching blade to stem
    • Leaf Axil — upper acute angle between petiole and stem where the axillary bud is found
    • Tendrils — leaves that attach to objects
    • Bulbs — leaves that store food
Tissues of Flowering Plants
  • Meristematic tissue enables flowering plants to grow throughout their lifetime
  • Apical meristems at the tips of stems and roots increase the length of these tissues; produces 3 types of meristem → 3 specialized tissues: Epidermal, Ground, Vascular
  • Epidermal Tissue — forms the outer protective covering of a plant
    • Epidermal cells exposed to air are covered with waxy cuticle
    • Root epidermal cells have root hairs
    • Epidermal cells of stems, leaves, and reproductive organs have trichomes
    • Lower leaf surface contains stomata
    • In older woody plants, epidermis of stem replaced by periderm; major component is cork; new cork made by cork cambium
  • Ground Tissue — forms bulk of a flowering plant
    • Parenchyma cells: least specialized; found in all organs of plant; can divide and give rise to more specialized cells
    • Collenchyma cells: have thicker primary walls; form bundles underneath epidermis; provide flexible support for immature regions of the plant
    • Sclerenchyma cells: have thick secondary walls impregnated with lignin; most are nonliving; primary function is to support mature regions of the plant; two types: fibers and sclereids
  • Vascular Tissue
    • Xylem transports water and minerals from the roots to the leaves
      • Tracheids: long, with tapered ends; water moves across pits in end walls and side walls; vascular rays between rows of tracheids conduct water across the width of the plant
      • Vessel Elements: larger, with perforated plates in end walls; form a continuous vessel for water and mineral transport
    • Phloem transports sucrose and other organic compounds from the leaves to the roots
      • Sieve-tube members: function as conducting cells; contain cytoplasm but lack nuclei; sieve plate = cluster of pores in wall
      • Each sieve-tube member has a companion cell; plasmodesmata connect the two; companion cell contains a nucleus
Memory hook"Every Good Vascular Plant" → Epidermal, Ground, Vascular · "X-press water Up" = Xylem · "Phloem = Phood (sugar) flows both ways"
Organization & Diversity of Roots
  • Root cap contains root apical meristem; zones from tip upward: zone of cell division → zone of elongation (cells lengthening and becoming specialized) → zone of maturation (fully differentiated cells)
  • Eudicot root tissues (outside → in): epidermis → cortex (parenchyma, allows water/mineral movement) → endodermis (Casparian strip; forms boundary between cortex and vascular cylinder) → pericycle (mitotically active; begins development of lateral roots) → vascular tissue (xylem and phloem)
  • Monocot roots: ground tissue of root's pith surrounded by vascular ring; same growth zones as eudicot but no secondary growth; have pericycle, endodermis, cortex, and epidermis
  • Root types:
    • Primary root (taproot): fleshy, long single root, grows straight down; stores food
    • Fibrous root system: slender roots and lateral branches; anchors plant to soil
    • Adventitious roots: develop from organs of the shoot system (e.g. prop roots)
  • Haustoria: found in parasitic plants; rootlike projections that grow into host plant; make contact with vascular tissue of host plant and extract water and nutrients
  • Mycorrhizae: mutualistic associations between roots and fungi; assist in water and mineral extraction
  • Root nodules: contain nitrogen-fixing bacteria
Organization & Diversity of Stems
  • Shoot apical meristem produces new cells that elongate and increase stem length; protected by terminal bud (enveloped by leaf primordia)
    • Protoderm → epidermis
    • Ground meristem → pith and cortex
    • Procambium → primary xylem and phloem
  • Herbaceous stems: mature non-woody; only primary growth; outermost tissue covered with waxy cuticle
    • Herbaceous eudicots: vascular bundles arranged in distinct ring
    • Monocots: vascular bundles scattered throughout stem
  • Woody stems: both primary and secondary growth; primary growth increases length; secondary growth increases girth; three distinct regions: bark, wood, pith
    • Bark: contains cork, cork cambium, and phloem; bark can be removed but harmful to plant (loss of organic nutrient transport); cork cells impregnated with suberin; gas exchange impeded except at lenticels
    • Wood: secondary xylem that builds up year after year; vascular cambium dormant in winter; annual ring = spring wood + summer wood; inner rings (heartwood) no longer function in water transport
  • Stem types: stolons (above-ground horizontal; produce new plants when nodes touch ground); rhizomes (underground horizontal; contribute to asexual reproduction); tubers (enlarged portions functioning in food storage); corms (underground stems that produce new plants during next season)
Organization & Diversity of Leaves
  • Leaves contain:
    • Upper and lower epidermis: waxy cuticle; trichomes; lower epidermis has stomata for gas exchange
    • Palisade mesophyll: elongated cells; contains many chloroplasts
    • Spongy mesophyll: irregular cells bounded by air spaces; contains many chloroplasts
  • Blade of a leaf can be simple or compound
  • Leaves are adapted to environmental conditions: shade leaves, spines, climbing leaves

7.2Plant Nutrition & Transport
Soil & Nutrition
  • Water and carbon dioxide are critical components of plant nutrition; 95% of plant's dry weight is C, H, or O
  • Other essential nutrients taken up by roots; minerals = inorganic substance usually containing 2 or more elements
    • Essential nutrients: 1) identifiable role in the plant, 2) cannot be replaced by another nutrient, 3) deficiency causes death
    • Broken down into macronutrients vs. micronutrients based on relative concentrations in plant tissue
  • Hydroponics = main method of determining essential nutrients; grow in presence and absence of each mineral
  • Soil = mixture of mineral particles, decaying organic matter, living organisms, air, and water
    • Sand: largest, most open space and drainage
    • Silt: intermediate size and drainage
    • Clay: smallest particles, hold water with little room for air
    • Humus: decaying organic matter; mixes with top layer; plants do well in soil containing minimum 10–20% humus
Water & Mineral Uptake
  • Water enters root hairs when the osmotic pressure in the root is lower than found in the soil (osmosis)
  • Minerals can be taken in at much higher concentration than found in soil by use of pumps to move ions
  • Water and minerals move up xylem, from root to leaves, by upward movement of water; minerals can exit xylem and enter cells requiring the nutrients along the way
  • Rhizobium bacteria: can live in root nodules on plants and break down nitrogen; receive carbohydrates from plant in return
  • Mycorrhizae: mutualistic between plant roots and fungus; almost ALL plants have mycorrhizae; increase surface area of root system; break down organic matter in soil, freeing up nutrients; root provides sugars and amino acids for fungus
  • Parasitic plants tap into other plant xylem (dodders, pinedrops); carnivorous plants capture and digest insects (Venus flytrap)
Transport Mechanisms — Water Potential & Xylem
  • Pressure potential: the effect that pressure has on water potential; water moves across a membrane from higher pressure to lower pressure; the higher the water pressure, the higher the water potential
  • Osmotic potential: takes into account the presence of solutes; water tends to move from lower solute concentration to higher solute concentration; lower concentration of solutes = higher water potential
  • Turgor pressure: pressure potential that increases due to osmosis
  • Xylem vessels form an open pipeline; vessel elements separated by perforated plates; water moves into and out of tracheids through pits
  • Root pressure: water entering roots creates positive pressure that pushes xylem sap upward; may be responsible for guttation (water forced out vein endings along edges of leaves)
  • Cohesion-tension model — passive xylem transport:
    • Cohesion = tendency of water molecules to cling together
    • Adhesion = ability of polar water molecules to interact with molecules of vessel walls
    • A continuous water column moves passively upward due to transpiration
    • Transpiration causes water loss through stomata; water molecules that evaporate are replaced by water molecules from leaf veins; due to cohesion, transpiration exerts a pulling force (tension) drawing water through the xylem to the leaf cells
    • Waxy cuticle prevents water loss when stomata are closed
Memory hookTACT: Transpiration → Adhesion → Cohesion → Tension pulls water up
Transport Mechanisms — Stomata
  • Each stoma in a leaf epidermis is bordered by guard cells
  • Increased turgor pressure in guard cells opens stoma
  • Active transport of K⁺ into guard cells causes water to enter by osmosis and stomata to open
  • H⁺ ions accumulate outside guard cells as K⁺ moves in
  • Opening and closing of stomata is regulated by light
  • ABA (abscisic acid) can also cause stomata to close
Transport Mechanisms — Phloem (Sugar)
  • Phloem transports sugar; phloem sap doesn't only move upward or downward as xylem does
  • Travels from source (sugar's origin) to sink (sugars are unloaded)
  • Evidence: girdling of tree below leaves causes bark to swell just above cut — sugar accumulates in swollen tissue; radioactive tracer studies confirm phloem transports organic nutrients; phloem sap can be collected using aphids
  • Pressure-flow model:
    • Sieve tubes form a continuous pathway; sieve-tube members aligned end to end; strands of plasmodesmata extend through sieve plates
    • Sucrose is actively transported into phloem at the leaves
    • Water follows by osmosis, creating positive pressure
    • Increase in pressure causes flow that moves water and sucrose from source to sink (from leaves down to roots for storage)
Key contrastXylem = passive (cohesion-tension, no energy needed). Phloem = active transport required at source to load sucrose.

7.3Plant Control of Growth Responses
Plant Hormones — Signal Transduction
  • Flowering plants respond to environmental stimuli: light, gravity, carbon dioxide levels, pathogen infection, drought, and touch
  • Responses can be short term (stomata open/close in response to light) or long term (gravity causes downward root growth and upward stem growth)
  • Response of plants to environmental stimuli involves signal transduction: the binding of a molecular "signal" that initiates and amplifies a response
    • Receptors: proteins activated by a specific signal; usually found on cell exterior
    • Transduction pathway: a series of relay proteins or enzymes that amplify and transform the signal to one understood by the machinery of the cell
    • Cellular response: the result of the transduction pathway
  • Hormones: chemical signal to coordinate cell responses; enable plant cells to communicate; usually synthesized in one part of the plant; travel within phloem or from cell to cell in response to appropriate stimulus
Memory hook — 5 hormones"A Gorgeous Cat Always Eats" → Auxin · Gibberellins · Cytokinins · ABA · Ethylene

Tropisms
  • Tropism: plant growth toward or away from a unidirectional stimulus; positive tropism = growth toward the stimulus; negative tropism = growth away from stimulus
  • Gravitropism: movement in response to gravity; when a plant is placed on its side, stem grows upward (opposite gravity); stems with root caps grow downward; response depends on sensors called statoliths (starch grains in amyloplasts that settle at gravity side of cell due to gravity); auxin may be responsible
  • Phototropism: movement in response to light; positive phototropism of stems occurs because cells on shady side elongate due to presence of auxin; a pigment in the cell membrane (phototropin) absorbs blue light and initiates phototropism
  • Thigmotropism: unusual growth due to contact with solid objects (e.g. coiling of tendrils); thigmomorphogenesis = entire plant responds to environmental stimuli (wind/rain)
  • Nastic movements: do not involve growth and are not dependent on stimulus direction; result from touch, shaking, or thermal stimulation; have to do with turgor pressure from water leaving and entering the cell (e.g. Mimosa pudica, Venus flytrap)
Photoperiodism, Phytochrome & Sleep Movements
  • Photoperiodism: any physiological response prompted by changes in day or night length; influences flowering in some plants; requires participation of a biological clock and a plant photoreceptor called phytochrome
  • Phytochrome: blue-green leaf pigment that alternately exists in two forms
    • Phytochrome red (Pr) = inactive; absorbs red light → converts to Pfr
    • Phytochrome far-red (Pfr) = active; absorbs far-red light → converts back to Pr
    • Conversion of forms allows plant to detect photoperiod changes
    • Also promotes seed germination and inhibits stem elongation
  • Flowering groups: Short-day plants flower when day length is shorter than a critical length; Long-day plants flower when day length is longer than a critical length; Day-neutral plants are not dependent on day length for flowering
  • Sleep movements: occur daily in response to light and dark changes; circadian rhythm = biological rhythms with a 24-hour cycle; tend to be persistent; rhythm is maintained in the absence of environmental stimuli; caused by a biological clock
Responses to Biotic Environment
  • Plants are always under attack by herbivores and parasites
  • Physical defenses: cuticle-covered epidermis and bark
  • Chemical defenses (secondary metabolites):
    • Tannins — tea leaves
    • Alkaloids — morphine, nicotine, caffeine
    • Cyanogenic glycosides — sugar-containing group that metabolizes into cyanide and inhibits cellular respiration
    • Wound responses: proteinase inhibitors and systemin produced in plant; triggered to release when a wound is made
    • Hypersensitive response (HR): initiates wound response and seals wounded area
    • Indirect defenses — prevent egg laying in insects
  • Mutualistic relationships: acacia tree and acacia ant — tree has swollen thorns; ants live in hollow interior, get nutrients, and protect the plant from predators

7.4Plant Reproduction
Reproductive Strategies — Life Cycle
  • Plants have a two-stage, alternating life cycle: sporophyte (2n) produces haploid spores by meiosis → spores divide mitotically to become haploid gametophytes → gametophytes produce gametes → gametes fuse to produce diploid zygote → zygote divides mitotically to become diploid sporophyte
  • A flower produces two types of spores:
    • Microspore (male gametophyte): undergoes mitosis → becomes pollen grain
    • Megaspore (female gametophyte): undergoes mitosis → becomes the female gametophyte (embryo sac) within an ovule within an ovary
  • Ovule → seed; Ovary → fruit
  • Flowering occurs in response to environmental signals such as day length; monocots: flower parts in threes and multiples of three; eudicots: flower parts in fours or fives
Flower Structure
  • A typical flower has four whorls of modified leaves attached to a receptacle at the end of a flower stalk called a peduncle:
    • Sepals: protect the bud
    • Petals: attract pollinators
    • Stamens (male portion): anther (saclike container) + filament (slender stalk)
    • Carpel (female portion): stigma (enlarged sticky knob) + style (slender stalk) + ovary (enlarged base enclosing ovules)
  • Complete flowers have sepals, petals, stamens, and a carpel; incomplete flowers are missing one or more
  • Perfect (bisexual) flowers have both stamens and carpels; imperfect (unisexual) flowers have one but not the other
  • Monoecious plants have staminate and carpellate flowers on the same plant; dioecious plants have them on separate plants (need 2 plants of different flowering types to get fruit)
Memory hook"Silly Puppies Sit (Pretty)" → Sepals · Petals · Stamens · (Pistil/Carpel)
Gametophyte Development
  • Male gametophytes: microspores produced in anthers; each anther has four pollen sacs, each with many microspore mother cells; microspore mother cells undergo meiosis to produce microspores; microspores undergo mitosis to produce pollen grains
  • Female gametophyte: ovary contains one or more ovules; one parenchyma cell enlarges to become megaspore mother cell; megaspore mother cell undergoes meiosis to produce 4 haploid megaspores (3 nonfunctional); functional megaspore divides mitotically until 8 nuclei in female gametophyte (embryo sac):
    • One egg cell associated with two synergid cells
    • One central cell with two polar nuclei
    • Three antipodal cells
Pollination & Double Fertilization
  • Pollination: transfer of pollen from an anther to the stigma of a carpel; self-pollination (same plant) or cross-pollination (different plant)
  • When pollen grain lands on stigma → germinates, forming a pollen tube; pollen tube passes between stigma and style to reach micropyle of the ovule
  • Double fertilization (unique to angiosperms!):
    • One sperm nucleus + egg nucleus → 2n zygote
    • Other sperm nucleus + polar nuclei → 3n endosperm nucleus → develops into endosperm
  • Mature seed contains the embryo, stored food, and seed coat
Seed Development
  • Eudicot embryo stages: proembryo + suspensor → globular → heart (cotyledons appear) → torpedo → mature embryo
    • Epicotyl: portion between cotyledons → contributes to shoot development
    • Hypocotyl: portion below → contributes to stem development
    • Radicle: the embryonic root
  • Monocots: cotyledon stores nutrients and absorbs from endosperm; eudicots: cotyledon stores all nutrients embryo will need
Fruit Types & Seed Dispersal
  • A fruit is a mature ovary; ovary wall thickens to become pericarp: exocarp (outermost skin) → mesocarp (fleshy tissue) → endocarp (boundary around seeds)
  • Simple fruits: from single ovary; Aggregate: ovaries from one flower on one receptacle (blackberry); Multiple: ovaries from separate clustered flowers (pineapple)
  • Dry fruits: dehiscent (splits when ripe — legumes) or indehiscent (does not split — grains)
  • Seed germination requirements: oxygen + adequate temperature + adequate moisture; some seeds need dormant period first
Asexual & Artificial Reproduction
  • Vegetative reproduction: new plant grows from fragment of parent plant or specialized reproductive structure; can be natural or induced artificially
  • Natural methods: stolons (horizontal stems), rhizomes (underground stems), cutting, grafting, mound layering, aerial layering
  • Lab-based:
    • Tissue culture: growth in artificial medium; plant cells are totipotent (each has genetic capability of becoming entire plant)
    • Somatic embryogenesis: hormones stimulate plantlets from leaf or other tissue
    • Anther tissue culture: haploid pollen cells cultured → haploid plantlets; chemical agent can double chromosomes → diploid
    • Cell suspension culture: calluses broken into liquid medium → extract chemicals/metabolites

7.5Animal Organization
4 Types of Animal Tissue
Memory hook"Every Cell Makes Noise" → Epithelial · Connective · Muscular · Nervous
  • Epithelial Tissue: forms continuous layer over body surfaces; exposed to environment on one side, connected to basement layer on other; lines body cavities; forms glands; functions: protection, secretion, absorption, excretion, filtration
    • Simple epithelia (single layer): squamous (flattened), cuboidal (cube-shaped), columnar (resembling columns)
    • Stratified epithelia: multiple layers; only bottom layer touching basement membrane
    • Glandular: exocrine (secrete into ducts/cavities); endocrine (secrete directly into bloodstream — pituitary, thyroid)
    • Cells connected by tight junctions, adhesion junctions, or gap junctions
  • Connective Tissue: specialized cells + ground substance + protein fibers (collagen — strength/flexibility; reticular — thinly branched collagen; elastic — contain elastin)
    • Fibrous: loose (allows organs to expand); adipose (stores energy, insulates); dense (tendons = muscle→bone; ligaments = bone→bone)
    • Supportive: cartilage (cells in lacunae — hyaline, elastic, fibrocartilage); bone (inorganic salts + protein; osteocytes in lacunae; osteons = concentric lamellae around central canals)
    • Fluid: blood (RBC + WBC + platelets in plasma); lymph (in lymphatic vessels)
  • Muscular Tissue: actin + myosin filaments; cells = muscle fibers
    • Skeletal: voluntary; long, striated fibers; multinucleated
    • Smooth (visceral): involuntary; no striations
    • Cardiac: striated but mostly involuntary; intercalated disks; pacemaker cells
  • Nervous Tissue: conducts electrical impulses
    • Neurons: dendrites → cell body → axon; outside CNS, fibers bound by CT form nerves
    • Neuroglia: support and nourish neurons (microglia, astrocytes, oligodendrocytes)
    • Functions: sensory input → data integration → motor output
Organs, Organ Systems & Body Cavities
  • Organ: 2+ tissue types working together for a particular function
  • Organ system: various organs cooperating to carry out a general process
  • Dorsal cavity: cranial cavity (brain) + vertebral canal (spinal cord)
  • Ventral cavity: thoracic (heart + lungs) + abdominal (most internal organs) + pelvic (certain reproductive organs)
Integumentary System
  • Functions: covers/protects body; regulates temperature; contains sensory receptors
  • Skin layers:
    • Epidermis: stratified squamous epithelium; cells pushed outward → keratinize → slough off; melanocytes produce melanin; UV → DNA mutations → skin cancer
    • Dermis: deeper and thicker; fibrous CT with elastic + collagen fibers; receptors, nerve fibers, blood vessels
    • Subcutaneous layer: loose CT below dermis
  • Accessory structures: nails (keratinized epithelial cells); hair follicles (begin in dermis; sebaceous glands secrete sebum); sweat glands (temperature regulation)
Homeostasis
  • Homeostasis: ability of an organism to maintain a relatively constant internal environment
  • Negative feedback: primary homeostatic mechanism; sensor detects change → regulatory center initiates action to bring conditions back to normal
  • Positive feedback: brings about ever greater change in same direction; does NOT result in equilibrium; less common; examples: childbirth, blood clotting, stomach protein digestion; can be problematic (fever)
  • Homeostatic control: partially by hormones; ultimately by nervous system
Key distinctionNegative = corrects back to set point. Positive = amplifies further. Positive is always the exception.

7.6Circulation & Cardiovascular System
Transport in Invertebrates
  • Two types of circulatory fluids: blood (in blood vessels) and hemolymph (mixture of blood and tissue fluid; fills body cavity)
  • Open circulatory system: heart pumps hemolymph via vessels; vessels empty into tissue spaces
  • Closed circulatory system: heart pumps blood to capillaries; gases and materials diffuse to/from nearby cells; vessels return blood to heart without contact between blood and tissues
Transport in Vertebrates
  • All vertebrates have a closed cardiovascular system; atria receive blood; ventricles pump blood out
  • Vessels: arteries (away from heart; thick walls; high pressure) → arterioles → capillaries (exchange; all cells within 60–80µm; only 5% open at one time) → venules → veins (return to heart; thin walls; have valves)
  • Fish: 1 loop, 1 atrium + 1 ventricle; Amphibians: 2 loops, 2 atria + 1 ventricle; Other vertebrates: 2 loops, 4 chambers (septum separates completely)
Human Heart Structure
  • Fist-sized, cone-shaped, cardiac muscle; in pericardium sac; behind sternum; apex tilted left
  • Septum divides left and right; atria (thin-walled; receive blood) and ventricles (thick-walled; pump blood)
  • Valves: tricuspid (3-flap, right atrium→ventricle); bicuspid/mitral (2-flap, left atrium→ventricle); pulmonary semilunar (right ventricle→pulmonary trunk); aortic semilunar (left ventricle→aorta)
Path of Blood Through the Heart
Deoxygenated — returning from systemic circuit: Body → Venae cavae → Right atrium → tricuspid → Right ventricle → pulmonary semilunar → Lungs Oxygenated — returning from pulmonary circuit: Lungs → Left atrium → bicuspid → Left ventricle → aortic semilunar → Body (aorta) Oxygen-poor blood never mixes with oxygen-rich blood. Blood pressure greater in aorta.
Memory hookRight side = deoxygenated → lungs. Left side = oxygenated → body. "Left does the heavy lifting."
Heartbeat & Cardiac Conduction
  • Systole = contraction; Diastole = relaxation
  • Cardiac cycle: atria fill → atria contract (push blood through AV valves) → ventricles contract (AV valves close = "Lub"; semilunar open) → ventricles relax (semilunar close = "Dub")
  • SA node (sinoatrial): pacemaker; keeps heartbeat regular
  • AV node: signals ventricles to contract
  • ECG: P wave = atria depolarize (about to contract); QRS = ventricles depolarize, atria relax; T wave = ventricular muscle fibers recover
  • Blood pressure: systolic (ventricular contraction) / diastolic (ventricular relaxation); measured with sphygmomanometer on brachial artery
Blood
  • RBC: biconcave disks; no nucleus; hemoglobin (4 globin chains + iron-containing heme groups → carries O₂); made in red bone marrow
  • Blood types: ABO + Rh systems; antibodies in plasma cause agglutination; Rh– mother + Rh+ father → potential antibody attack on subsequent Rh+ pregnancy
  • WBC — Granular: neutrophils (phagocytize bacteria); basophils (histamine); eosinophils (fight parasitic worms). Agranular: monocytes (→ macrophages); lymphocytes (T and B cells)
  • Platelets: from megakaryocytes; blood clot = RBCs + fibrin threads
  • Capillary exchange: O₂ and nutrients diffuse out; CO₂ and wastes diffuse in; excess tissue fluid → lymphatic capillaries → returns to venous blood at shoulder
Cardiovascular Disease
  • Hypertension: high blood pressure
  • Atherosclerosis: fatty materials accumulate between inner linings of arteries
  • Stroke: cranial arteriole bursts or blocked by embolus
  • Heart attack (myocardial infarction): coronary artery completely blocked
  • Angina pectoris: painful squeezing from myocardial O₂ insufficiency (partial blockage)

7.7Respiratory System
Gas Exchange Surfaces
  • Respiration steps: ventilation → external respiration (lungs↔blood) → internal respiration (blood↔tissues) → cellular respiration (mitochondria)
  • Aquatic animals expend more energy for gas exchange: fish 25%, humans 1–2%; water more dense, carries fraction of oxygen of air
  • Small aquatic (hydra, planarian): large surface area; cells exchange directly with environment
  • Earthworm: body surface (capillaries near surface)
  • Fish gills: highly vascularized; countercurrent exchange — water flows opposite to oxygen-depleted blood → up to 90% O₂ extraction (concurrent would equilibrate at ~50%)
  • Insects: tracheal system (trachea → tubules → tracheoles); delivers air directly to cells; air sacs act as bellows
  • Human lungs: ~70m² surface area (50× skin); air dried effect so lungs kept moistened
Human Respiratory Anatomy
Nose/Mouth→Pharynx→Glottis→Larynx (voice box)→Trachea (windpipe)→Bronchi (2)→Bronchioles→Alveoli Filtered, warmed, humidified along way. Larynx and trachea held open by cartilage rings. Epiglottis covers glottis when swallowing.
Breathing Mechanics
  • Inspiration: diaphragm contracts (lowers) + rib cage rises → thoracic volume ↑ → pressure below atmospheric → air flows in
  • Expiration: diaphragm rises + rib cage lowers → thoracic volume ↓ → pressure above atmospheric → air flows out
  • Tidal ventilation (all terrestrial vertebrates except birds): air in and out same route; lungs never fully emptied; new air mixes with old → less efficient but conserves water
  • Birds: one-way ventilation → fresh air never mixes with used air → more efficient
  • Normal adults: 12–20 ventilations/min; rhythm controlled by medulla oblongata
Gas Exchange & Transport
  • Gases diffuse from high → low partial pressure
  • Alveoli: higher O₂, lower CO₂ partial pressure than pulmonary blood → O₂ enters blood, CO₂ exits; opposite occurs in tissues
  • External respiration: O₂ + Hb → oxyhemoglobin (HbO₂)
  • Internal respiration: HbO₂ releases O₂ to tissues; most CO₂ transported as bicarbonate ion (HCO₃⁻); some binds Hb → carbaminohemoglobin (HbCO₂)
Respiratory Disorders
  • Upper tract: cold (viral; antibiotics ineffective); strep throat (Streptococcus pyogenes; antibiotics work)
  • Lower tract: acute bronchitis (infection of bronchi); chronic bronchitis (airways inflamed, filled with mucus); asthma (bronchiole smooth muscles spasm)
  • Lungs: pneumonia (bronchi and alveoli fill with fluid); tuberculosis (Mycobacterium tuberculosis); pulmonary fibrosis (fibrous CT builds up); emphysema (alveoli distended, walls damaged → reduced surface area); lung cancer (thickening → atypical cells → metastasize)

7.8Nervous System
Evolution & Organization
  • Hydras: nerve net; planarians: ladderlike; cephalization = concentration of ganglia and sensory receptors in head; annelids/arthropods/molluscs: true nervous systems
  • Vertebrate brain: hindbrain, midbrain, forebrain; CNS develops from embryonic dorsal neural tube
  • CNS: brain + spinal cord
  • PNS: all nerves and ganglia outside CNS; somatic (controls skeletal muscle; voluntary); autonomic (smooth muscle, cardiac, glands; involuntary → sympathetic + parasympathetic)
Nervous Tissue & Action Potential
  • Neuron: dendrites (receive signals) → cell body → axon (conducts impulse; covered by myelin sheath)
  • Motor (efferent): CNS → muscles/glands; Sensory (afferent): receptors → CNS; Interneurons: within CNS
  • Resting potential: –70 mV; inside more negative (Na⁺ outside, K⁺ inside; maintained by Na⁺/K⁺ pump)
  • Action potential: stimulus above threshold → Na⁺ rushes in (depolarization) → K⁺ rushes out (repolarization) → refractory period (Na⁺ channels unable to open)
  • All-or-nothing event; intensity = frequency of impulses, not magnitude
  • Myelinated: saltatory (jumping) conduction — action potential jumps node to node → faster
Memory hookNa⁺ IN = depolarization (going positive). K⁺ OUT = repolarization (back to –70mV).
Transmission Across a Synapse
  • Synapse: neurons nearly touch; gap = synaptic cleft; transmission by neurotransmitters
  • Ca²⁺ rise in axon terminal → vesicles merge with presynaptic membrane → neurotransmitters released into cleft → bind postsynaptic receptors
  • Synaptic integration: sum of all excitatory and inhibitory signals; must cross threshold to propagate action potential
The CNS — Brain & Spinal Cord
  • CNS functions: receives sensory input, performs integration, generates motor output
  • Brain and spinal cord wrapped in meninges; spaces filled with cerebrospinal fluid
  • Spinal cord: reflex center + communication between brain and spinal nerves; gray matter (cell bodies, unmyelinated) + white matter (myelinated tracts)

Peripheral Nervous System
  • Somatic: cranial and spinal nerves; controls skeletal muscles; voluntary
  • Autonomic: smooth muscles, cardiac, glands; involuntary; 2 neurons + 1 ganglion per impulse
    • Sympathetic: fight-or-flight; accelerates heartbeat, dilates bronchi; neurotransmitter = norepinephrine
    • Parasympathetic: rest-and-digest; promotes digestion, retards heartbeat; neurotransmitter = acetylcholine
  • Reflex arc: sensory receptor → sensory neuron → dorsal root ganglion → interneuron in spinal cord → motor neuron → effector

7.9Sense Organs
Sensory Receptors
  • Sensory receptors: specialized cells detecting changes in internal/external conditions; communicate to CNS; facilitate sensory transduction (stimulus → nerve impulse)
  • No difference between nerve impulses from different sensory nerves; perceptions happen in the brain
  • Chemoreceptors (taste, smell); Photoreceptors (light); Mechanoreceptors (pressure, sound, touch); Thermoreceptors (temperature)
Chemical Senses (Taste & Smell)
  • Chemoreception: most primitive sense; allows organisms to locate food, find mates, detect dangerous chemicals
  • Taste buds on tongue; open at taste pore; 5 primary tastes: sweet, sour, salty, bitter, umami (savory)
  • Smell: olfactory cells in olfactory epithelium (roof of nasal cavity) → olfactory bulb → cerebral cortex; taste and smell work together; both interpreted by cerebral cortex
Vision — The Human Eye
  • Sclera (opaque outer; cornea in front; conjunctiva covers sclera) → Choroid (blood vessels; iris regulates pupil; lens forms images) → Retina (photoreceptors)
  • Ciliary muscle controls lens shape: distant = relaxed; near = contracted
  • Rods: low light, peripheral vision, motion; pigment = rhodopsin (opsin + retinal/vitamin A)
  • Cones: bright light, fine detail, color; 3 types: B (blue), G (green), R (red)
  • Fovea centralis: densest cone concentration → sharpest vision; blind spot = optic nerve exit (no receptors)
  • Retina layers: rods/cones → bipolar cells → ganglion cells (initiate impulses); ~150M rods, ~6M cones, ~1M ganglion cells
Hearing — The Human Ear
  • Outer ear: pinna + auditory canal
  • Middle ear: tympanic membrane → ossicles (malleus, incus, stapes) → oval window; eustachian tube connects to nasopharynx
  • Inner ear: semicircular canals (rotational equilibrium), vestibule (gravitational equilibrium), cochlea (hearing)
  • Sound path: auditory canal → tympanic membrane → malleus → incus → stapes → oval window → cochlea fluid → basilar membrane vibrates → hair cells in organ of Corti bend → cochlear nerve → brain stem → auditory cortex
  • Fish: lateral line system (detects water currents and pressure waves)
OssiclesMalleus (hammer) · Incus (anvil) · Stapes (stirrup) — MIS
Balance & Somatic Senses
  • Rotational equilibrium: ampullae of semicircular canals (hair cells shift during rotation → vestibular nerve)
  • Gravitational equilibrium: utricle and saccule (hair cells shift when head bends → vestibular nerve)
  • Somatic senses: receptors in skin, muscles, joints, viscera
    • Proprioceptors: muscle tone (muscle spindles, Golgi tendon organs)
    • Cutaneous: Meissner corpuscles, Merkel disks, Pacinian corpuscles, Ruffini endings, Krause end bulbs
    • Nociceptors (free nerve endings): pain; damaged cells release chemicals → brain interprets as pain

7.10Locomotion & Support Systems
Diversity of Skeletons
  • Skeletons: rigidity, protection, surface for muscle attachment
  • Hydrostatic skeleton: fluid pressure offers support; some use gastrovascular cavity, others use coelom; cnidarians, flatworms, roundworms, annelids
  • Exoskeleton: external; mollusks (calcium carbonate); arthropods (chitin); must molt to grow
  • Endoskeleton: internal; echinoderms + vertebrates; mineralized bone and cartilage; grows with animal; doesn't limit internal organs; supports greater weight
Human Skeletal System
  • Functions: support; organ protection; muscle attachment; ion storage (Ca²⁺ and phosphate); blood cell production (marrow of skull, ribs, sternum, pelvis, long bones)
  • Bone cells: osteoblasts (build bone); osteoclasts (break down bone; deposit Ca²⁺ in blood; work with osteoblasts to heal fractures); osteocytes (bone cells in lacunae at lamellar boundaries)
  • Endochondral ossification: cartilage model → Ca²⁺ deposited by osteoblasts; controlled by growth and sex hormones
  • Long bone: compact bone (outer cylinder; osteons = concentric lamellae around central canal; osteocytes in lacunae) + medullary cavity (yellow marrow/fat) + spongy bone at ends (red marrow, blood cell production)
Memory hookBlast = Build. Clast = Crumble (osteoclast breaks bone down).
Axial vs. Appendicular Skeleton
  • Axial (midline): skull (cranium + facial bones; foramen magnum = spinal cord entrance) + vertebral column (7 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, sacrum, coccyx; intervertebral disks of fibrocartilage) + sternum + 12 pairs of ribs (7 true = connect to sternum; 5 false = don't)
  • Appendicular: pectoral girdle (shoulder; supports arms) + arms/hands + pelvic girdle (supports legs) + legs/feet
  • Joints: fibrous (immovable — cranial); cartilaginous (slightly movable — vertebrae); synovial (freely movable — knee/elbow = hinged; shoulder/hip = ball-and-socket; ligaments bind bones)
Muscular System
  • Skeletal muscles attached by tendons; antagonistic pairs (can only pull, not push; when one contracts it stretches partner); tone = resting minimal contraction; tetany = maximum sustained contraction
  • Microscopic: fibers → myofibrils (in sarcoplasmic reticulum = modified ER, stores Ca²⁺) → sarcomeres (units of contraction; myosin = thick filaments with cross-bridges; actin = thin filaments; Z-plates = boundaries)
  • Sliding filament model: nerve impulse → ACh released at neuromuscular junction → Ca²⁺ released from SR → Ca²⁺ binds troponin on actin → exposes myosin binding sites → myosin cross-bridges pull actin inward → Z-plates move together → sarcomere shortens → myosin hydrolyzes ATP to release → process continues until nerve stimulation ends → acetylcholinesterase breaks down ACh, ending contraction
